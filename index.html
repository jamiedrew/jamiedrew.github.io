Snack Man

In October 2021, the Bureau of Investigative Journalism broke a story about commercial cheese production (like the kind you get in Tesco) causing massive deforestation in Brazil.

The timing aligned wonderfully with COP26, the UN's climate change conference in Glasgow, and so with a very quick turnaround -- just two weeks -- we made a small Pac-Man-like game in which you play as a Babybel wreaking havoc in the rainforest.

A huge challenge was the delivery of the game in such a small timeframe -- there wasn't enough time to get it through Apple or Google's app store policies. The conference venue is also well-known for being a cold spot for mobile phone signals.

So we utilised Unity's experimental Project Tiny entity-component system and kept the game's footprint, well, tiny, so that conference attendees could play this game on their phones between talks.

The game was built with touch controls in mind; players only have to swipe to guide the little Edam through its mazes. The game was a hit at the conference in terms of downloads, but I personally made several enemies because of level five.

Crimson Shrine

A remake of a beloved game that doesn't exist.

For Hallowe'en 2020 I made a first-person shooter inspired by the sorely-missed TimeSplitters games; the stories of Robert W.Chambers, James Tiptree Jr., MR James, etc.; and Halo 3's "Ghost of Lockout."

Crimson Shrine is a "king of the hill"-type game in which players take control of a central point - the Shrine itself, surrounded by dark, narrow hallways and looked over by an ominous figure reaching from an uncanny doorway overlooking the abyss.

I say "players," but this is actually a single-player experience; the game is one part of a multimedia horror story told in part through the game's dev logs, its source code, and is intended to take place in conversation with the player, as opposed to simply delivering a linear narrative.

It's designed to be played over 10-15 minutes; horror outstays its welcome pretty quickly.

You've been assigned the poorly-scanned case of the Seabrooks, a family in a small Northern town who reported bumps in the night almost 20 years ago.

I wanted to make a cyberpunk story, but not a neon-and-rainslick cyberpunk story; I wanted to write the kind of cyberpunk that dresses itself in chinos and sits down for eight hours a day to spy on you and sell your darkest secrets to advertisers.

The Incident... is inspired by two degrees in linguistics and the hours I spent transcribing audio and making sense of historical documents, as well as a couple of years working part-time in audio transcription. These weren't bad jobs, exactly, but it's hard not to get invested in the lives of the people you're listening to and reading about, and it feels sort of unfair that they don't even know you exist.

Initially there was a more explicit tutorial around the game's mechanics, but I feel like if I had to suffer badly-written instructions then so do you.

Within the first month of its release more than 500 people played The Incident at the Seabrook Residence, a lot of them more than once. There's a grading system in place that tracks how well you "performed" and unlocks a secret ending with a grade of A or above.

The game is set in the last days of 2003, but a lot of players in 2020 resonated with that secret ending, in which one of the characters pushes back against your state-sanctioned invasion of her privacy.
